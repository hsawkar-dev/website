How Aquablation works

Aquablation pairs real-time ultrasound imaging with a computer-controlled robotic arm to map and remove the obstructing prostate tissue using a high-velocity saline waterjet. Because the waterjet uses no heat, the risk of thermal injury to the surrounding nerves and sphincters is minimized.

The robotic system carries out the plan with sub-millimeter accuracy every time, regardless of prostate size or complexity, which makes it especially well suited to large or complex prostates. Dr. Sawkar performs more Aquablation procedures than any surgeon in Orange County.

01

Anesthesia

The procedure is done in the hospital under spinal or general anesthesia, typically with one night's stay.

02

Ultrasound mapping

A transrectal ultrasound builds a 3D map of the prostate, defining exactly which tissue to remove.

03

Waterjet resection

A heat-free saline waterjet removes the planned tissue with robotic, sub-millimeter precision.

04

Recovery

A catheter stays in for a day or two. Most men return to normal activities within one to two weeks.
